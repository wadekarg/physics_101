Free Fall

Motion under the sole influence of gravity near Earth's surface.

Theory

Free fall is the motion of an object under the influence of gravity alone, with no air resistance or other forces acting on it. Near Earth's surface, the acceleration due to gravity is approximately g = 9.8 m/s² directed downward. This acceleration is the same for all objects regardless of their mass — a result famously demonstrated by Galileo and later confirmed dramatically on the Moon by astronaut David Scott, who dropped a hammer and a feather simultaneously and watched them land together.

In free fall, the kinematic equations apply with a = −g (taking upward as positive). For an object dropped from rest: v = −gt and y = −½gt². For an object thrown upward with initial velocity v₀, it decelerates at rate g, reaches a maximum height at time t = v₀/g, and the maximum height is h = v₀²/(2g). The total time in the air for a round trip (launched and landing at the same height) is T = 2v₀/g.

In reality, most objects experience air resistance (drag), which opposes their motion and depends on velocity, cross-sectional area, shape, and air density. As a falling object speeds up, drag force increases until it equals the gravitational force, at which point the net force is zero and the object falls at a constant speed called the terminal velocity. For a skydiver, terminal velocity is roughly 55 m/s (about 200 km/h) in a spread-eagle position. Objects with large surface area relative to their mass (like feathers) have low terminal velocities, while dense, streamlined objects (like a steel ball) have high terminal velocities and behave more like ideal free fall.
