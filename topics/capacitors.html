Capacitors

Devices that store electrical energy by accumulating charge on separated conducting plates.

Theory

A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy by holding equal and opposite charges on two conductors separated by an insulator (dielectric). Capacitance is defined as the ratio of charge stored to voltage applied: C = Q/V, measured in farads (F), where 1 F = 1 C/V. For a parallel-plate capacitor, the capacitance is C = ε₀A/d, where A is the plate area, d is the separation, and ε₀ is the permittivity of free space. Larger plates or smaller separation yield greater capacitance.

The energy stored in a capacitor is U = ½CV² = ½Q²/C = ½QV. This energy is stored in the electric field between the plates, with energy density u = ½ε₀E². Inserting a dielectric material (insulator) between the plates increases the capacitance by a factor κ (the dielectric constant): C = κε₀A/d. Common dielectrics include paper, ceramic, and plastic. The dielectric also increases the maximum voltage the capacitor can withstand before breakdown.

Capacitors can be combined in series (same charge, voltages add: 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...) or in parallel (same voltage, charges add: C_total = C₁ + C₂ + ...). Note this is the opposite pattern from resistors. Capacitors are ubiquitous in electronics: they smooth power supply voltages, filter signals, store energy for camera flashes, tune radio circuits, and serve as memory elements in DRAM chips. Understanding capacitors bridges electrostatics and circuit analysis.
