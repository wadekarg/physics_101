Gyroscopes & Precession

How spinning objects resist changes to their axis and exhibit the surprising phenomenon of precession.

Theory

A gyroscope is a spinning object that exhibits remarkable stability due to conservation of angular momentum. When a wheel spins rapidly, its angular momentum vector ⃗L = Iω points along the spin axis. According to τ = dL/dt, changing the direction of the angular momentum vector requires a torque. A large angular momentum means a large torque is needed to change the spin axis, which is why a spinning top stays upright and a bicycle is easier to balance when moving.

Precession occurs when a torque is applied perpendicular to the angular momentum vector. Instead of tipping over, the spin axis slowly rotates (precesses) around a vertical axis. The precession rate is Ω_p = τ / (Iω), where τ is the torque (often from gravity), I is the moment of inertia, and ω is the spin angular velocity. Faster spinning means slower precession. This counterintuitive behavior — the gyroscope moving sideways instead of falling — is a direct consequence of the vector nature of angular momentum.

Gyroscopic effects are used extensively in navigation and engineering. Gyrocompasses in ships and aircraft use spinning rotors to find true north. Ring laser gyroscopes in modern aircraft measure rotation rates for inertial navigation. The Earth itself precesses like a giant gyroscope — its spin axis traces a circle in the sky over about 26,000 years due to the gravitational torque from the Sun and Moon. This axial precession gradually changes which star is the "North Star."
