AC Circuits & RLC Resonance

The behavior of resistors, capacitors, and inductors in alternating current circuits.

Theory

In AC circuits, the voltage and current oscillate sinusoidally. A resistor in an AC circuit has current and voltage in phase. A capacitor has current leading voltage by 90°, with capacitive reactance X_C = 1/(2πfC) acting like a frequency-dependent resistance. An inductor has current lagging voltage by 90°, with inductive reactance X_L = 2πfL. At high frequencies, capacitors pass current easily while inductors block it; at low frequencies, the reverse is true.

In an RLC series circuit (resistor, inductor, capacitor), the total opposition to current is the impedance: Z = √(R² + (X_L − X_C)²). The current amplitude is I = V/Z, and the phase angle between voltage and current is tan φ = (X_L − X_C)/R. Average power dissipated is P = I_rms V_rms cos φ, where cos φ is the power factor. Only the resistor dissipates power; capacitors and inductors alternately store and release energy.

Resonance in an RLC circuit occurs when X_L = X_C, giving the resonance frequency f₀ = 1/(2π√(LC)). At resonance, impedance is minimum (Z = R), current is maximum, and the circuit behaves like a pure resistor. RLC resonance is the principle behind radio tuning — by adjusting the capacitance, you change the resonant frequency to match the desired radio station. RLC circuits are also used in filters (low-pass, high-pass, band-pass), oscillators, and signal processing throughout electronics and telecommunications.
