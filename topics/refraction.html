Refraction & Snell's Law

How light bends when passing between different transparent materials.

Theory

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another, caused by a change in the speed of light. The index of refraction (n) of a medium is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed in the medium: n = c/v. For example, n = 1.00 for air, n = 1.33 for water, and n = 1.50 for typical glass. Light slows down in denser media (higher n) and bends toward the normal when entering a denser medium.

Snell's Law quantifies refraction: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, where θ₁ and θ₂ are the angles of incidence and refraction measured from the normal. When light passes from a denser medium to a less dense medium (e.g., glass to air), it bends away from the normal. If the angle of incidence exceeds the critical angle (θ_c = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁)), the light is completely reflected back — this is total internal reflection.

Total internal reflection is the principle behind fiber optics: light bounces along the inside of a glass fiber with virtually no loss, enabling high-speed internet and medical endoscopes. Refraction causes familiar phenomena: a straw appears bent in a glass of water, swimming pools look shallower than they are, and dispersion (the dependence of n on wavelength) causes white light to separate into a rainbow when passing through a prism. Diamonds sparkle because of their high index of refraction (n = 2.42) and strong dispersion, combined with total internal reflection from many facets.
