Potential Energy & Conservation

Energy stored in position or configuration, and the fundamental law of energy conservation.

Theory

Potential energy is energy stored by virtue of an object's position or configuration within a force field. Gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is PE_g = mgh, where h is the height above a chosen reference point. Elastic potential energy stored in a spring is PE_s = ½kx², where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium. Both forms of potential energy are associated with conservative forces — forces for which the work done depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken.

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. For a system with only conservative forces, the total mechanical energy is constant: KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f. A ball thrown upward converts kinetic energy to gravitational potential energy as it rises, then converts it back as it falls. A mass on a spring oscillates as energy transforms between kinetic and elastic potential energy.

When non-conservative forces like friction are present, mechanical energy is not conserved — some is converted to thermal energy (heat). The generalized energy equation becomes: KE_i + PE_i + W_nc = KE_f + PE_f, where W_nc is the work done by non-conservative forces (usually negative for friction). Despite this, the total energy of the universe is always conserved. Conservation of energy is one of the most fundamental and far-reaching principles in all of physics, applying from subatomic particles to galaxies.
