Position, Velocity & Acceleration

The fundamental quantities that describe how objects move through space and time.

Theory

Position describes where an object is located relative to a chosen origin. When an object moves, the change in position is called displacement (Δx = x₂ − x₁), which is a vector quantity — it has both magnitude and direction. This is different from distance, which is the total path length traveled and is always positive. For example, if you walk 3 m east and then 3 m west, your distance traveled is 6 m, but your displacement is 0 m.

Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time. Average velocity is defined as v̄ = Δx / Δt, while instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific moment: v = dx/dt. Speed is the magnitude of velocity and is always non-negative. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity: a = dv/dt = d²x/dt². An object can be accelerating even while moving at constant speed — for example, a car turning a corner has changing velocity direction and therefore nonzero acceleration.

Understanding the relationships among position, velocity, and acceleration is the foundation of kinematics. If you know an object's acceleration as a function of time, you can integrate to find velocity, and integrate again to find position. Conversely, differentiating position gives velocity, and differentiating velocity gives acceleration. These relationships are central to all of classical mechanics and will appear repeatedly throughout your study of physics.
