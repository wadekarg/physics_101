Newton's Third Law — Action/Reaction

Every action has an equal and opposite reaction — forces always come in pairs.

Theory

Newton's Third Law states: for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. More precisely, if object A exerts a force on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts a force on object A that is equal in magnitude but opposite in direction: ⃗F_AB = −⃗F_BA. These are called action-reaction pairs (or Newton's Third Law pairs). It is important to note that the two forces in a pair always act on different objects — they never cancel each other out.

A common source of confusion: if the Earth pulls you down with gravity, and you pull the Earth up with equal force, why don't these cancel? Because they act on different objects. Your weight (Earth pulling you) and the normal force (floor pushing you up) are not a Third Law pair — they both act on you. The actual Third Law pair to your weight is the gravitational force you exert on the Earth. Since the Earth has enormous mass, its acceleration from your pull is negligibly small: a_Earth = F/M_Earth ≈ 0.

Third Law pairs are present in every interaction: when you push a wall, the wall pushes you back; when a rocket expels exhaust gases backward, the gases push the rocket forward; when a swimmer pushes water backward, the water pushes the swimmer forward. The normal force is a reaction force that surfaces exert perpendicular to themselves when objects press against them. Understanding Third Law pairs is crucial for correctly identifying forces and drawing free-body diagrams.
