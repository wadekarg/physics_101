Energy Levels & Spectra

How atoms can only exist in discrete energy states, producing unique spectral fingerprints.

Theory

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a model of the hydrogen atom where the electron can only occupy certain discrete orbits, each with a specific energy. The allowed energy levels are E_n = −13.6/n² eV, where n = 1, 2, 3, ... is the principal quantum number. The lowest energy state (n = 1) is the ground state with E = −13.6 eV. The energy is negative because the electron is bound to the nucleus — energy must be added to free it. The ionization energy of hydrogen is 13.6 eV.

When an electron transitions from a higher energy level to a lower one, it emits a photon with energy equal to the difference: E_photon = hf = E_i − E_f = 13.6(1/n_f² − 1/n_i²) eV. This produces an emission spectrum of discrete wavelengths unique to each element — an atomic fingerprint. The hydrogen spectrum has named series: the Lyman series (transitions to n = 1, ultraviolet), Balmer series (to n = 2, visible light), and Paschen series (to n = 3, infrared). Conversely, atoms absorb photons only at these same specific wavelengths, producing an absorption spectrum with dark lines.

The Bohr model was a crucial step toward modern quantum mechanics, correctly predicting hydrogen's spectrum. However, it fails for multi-electron atoms and cannot explain fine structure or molecular bonding. The full quantum mechanical model replaces orbits with orbitals — probability distributions described by quantum numbers n, l, and m_l. Despite its limitations, the concept of quantized energy levels is universally valid and explains emission/absorption spectra, lasers (stimulated emission between levels), fluorescence, and the operation of LEDs. Spectroscopy remains one of the most powerful tools in chemistry, astronomy, and materials science.
