E=mc² & Relativistic Effects

The equivalence of mass and energy, and how momentum and energy behave at relativistic speeds.

Theory

Einstein's most famous equation, E = mc², reveals that mass and energy are equivalent — two forms of the same thing. The rest energy of an object is the energy associated with its mass: even a small mass corresponds to an enormous amount of energy because c² is a huge number (c² = 9 × 10¹⁶ m²/s²). For example, 1 kg of matter contains 9 × 10¹⁶ J of rest energy — equivalent to about 21 megatons of TNT. This is the energy source of nuclear weapons and nuclear power plants.

The total energy of a particle is E = γmc², which includes both rest energy and kinetic energy. The relativistic kinetic energy is KE = (γ − 1)mc², which reduces to ½mv² at low speeds. The relativistic momentum is p = γmv, which increases without bound as v approaches c. This means no massive object can reach the speed of light — it would require infinite energy. The complete energy-momentum relation is E² = (pc)² + (mc²)².

For massless particles like photons, m = 0 and the relation gives E = pc. Photons have momentum despite having no mass, which is confirmed by radiation pressure and the Compton effect. Mass-energy equivalence explains the mass defect in nuclear reactions: the products have slightly less mass than the reactants, and the "missing" mass appears as kinetic energy of the products via ΔE = Δmc². This principle underlies nuclear fission (splitting heavy atoms), nuclear fusion (combining light atoms), and even the energy released in chemical reactions (though the mass change is immeasurably small for chemistry). E=mc² stands as one of the most profound and far-reaching equations in all of science.
