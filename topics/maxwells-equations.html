Maxwell's Equations & EM Waves

The four elegant equations that unify electricity, magnetism, and light into one framework.

Theory

Maxwell's Equations are four fundamental equations that describe all classical electromagnetic phenomena. Gauss's Law for electricity (∮⃗E · d⃗A = Q/ε₀) relates electric fields to charges. Gauss's Law for magnetism (∮⃗B · d⃗A = 0) states there are no magnetic monopoles. Faraday's Law (∮⃗E · d⃗l = −dΦ_B/dt) shows changing magnetic fields create electric fields. Ampère-Maxwell's Law (∮⃗B · d⃗l = μ₀I + μ₀ε₀ dΦ_E/dt) shows that both currents and changing electric fields create magnetic fields.

Maxwell's crucial addition was the displacement current term (μ₀ε₀ dΦ_E/dt) in Ampère's Law. This term means that a changing electric field produces a magnetic field, just as a changing magnetic field produces an electric field (Faraday's Law). This mutual creation allows electromagnetic disturbances to propagate through space as self-sustaining waves. Maxwell showed that these electromagnetic waves travel at speed c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3 × 10⁸ m/s — the speed of light. This revealed that light itself is an electromagnetic wave.

EM waves are transverse waves with oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. They carry energy (described by the Poynting vector ⃗S = (1/μ₀)⃗E × ⃗B) and momentum, and require no medium to travel. Maxwell's equations represent one of the greatest achievements in physics, unifying electricity, magnetism, and optics into a single elegant theory and paving the way for radio, television, radar, wireless communications, and our understanding of light and radiation.
