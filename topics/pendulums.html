Pendulums

The classic oscillating system that helped define timekeeping for centuries.

Theory

A simple pendulum consists of a mass (bob) suspended from a string of length L, swinging under the influence of gravity. For small angles (less than about 15°), the motion is approximately simple harmonic, with period T = 2π√(L/g). This remarkable result shows that the period depends only on the length of the pendulum and the local gravitational acceleration — not on the mass of the bob or the amplitude of the swing. This property made pendulums the basis of accurate clocks for centuries.

The restoring force for a pendulum is the component of gravity tangent to the arc: F = −mg sin θ. For small θ, sin θ ≈ θ (in radians), so the force becomes approximately linear: F ≈ −mgθ = −(mg/L)s, where s is the arc length. This is mathematically identical to Hooke's Law with an effective spring constant k_eff = mg/L. A physical pendulum is any rigid body pivoted at a point other than its center of mass, with period T = 2π√(I/mgh), where I is the moment of inertia about the pivot and h is the distance from pivot to center of mass.

In reality, pendulums experience damping due to air resistance and friction at the pivot, causing the amplitude to gradually decrease over time. The amplitude decays exponentially: A(t) = A₀ e^(−γt), where γ is the damping coefficient. If energy is continuously supplied to compensate for damping (as in a grandfather clock's escapement mechanism), the pendulum maintains a constant amplitude. Pendulums have been used to measure g, demonstrate Earth's rotation (Foucault pendulum), and even detect gravitational waves in early experiments.
