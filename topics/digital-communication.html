Digital Communication & Information

How analog signals are sampled, quantized, and encoded into binary — the foundation of all modern data transmission.

Theory

An analogue signal varies continuously in time and amplitude. A digital signal takes only discrete values (typically 0 and 1). Converting analogue to digital involves three steps. Sampling: measure the signal value at regular time intervals. The Nyquist theorem states that to faithfully reconstruct a signal of maximum frequency f_m, the sampling rate must be at least f_s ≥ 2f_m. Sampling below this rate causes aliasing — high frequencies appear as spurious low-frequency artefacts.

Quantisation rounds each sample to the nearest of 2^n discrete levels, where n is the bit depth. A CD uses n = 16 bits, giving 65,536 levels and 96 dB dynamic range. The rounding error is called quantisation noise; it decreases as 2^n increases. Encoding converts each quantised value to a binary number. This three-step process — Sample, Quantise, Encode — is called Pulse Code Modulation (PCM), the standard for telephone calls, audio CDs, and digital broadcasting.

Digital communication has decisive advantages over analogue: noise can be completely eliminated by signal regeneration (regenerators detect the 0/1 pattern and retransmit a perfect copy before noise accumulates); signals can be encrypted, compressed, and error-corrected; and they are directly compatible with computers. Shannon's channel capacity theorem: C = B log₂(1 + S/N), where B is bandwidth and S/N is the signal-to-noise ratio, sets the absolute maximum data rate for any channel — a result that guides the design of every WiFi router, 5G base station, and optical fibre network on Earth.
