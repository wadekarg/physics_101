p-n Junction Diode

The fundamental building block of all semiconductor devices — a one-way valve for electric current.

Theory

When p-type and n-type semiconductors are joined, electrons from the n-side diffuse across the boundary and recombine with holes on the p-side. This creates the depletion region — a thin zone (~1 μm) stripped of free carriers but containing fixed, ionised impurity atoms. The ionised donors on the n-side are positive; the ionised acceptors on the p-side are negative. This charge imbalance creates a built-in electric field (pointing from n to p) and a built-in potential of about 0.7 V for silicon that opposes further diffusion.

In forward bias, the positive terminal of the battery is connected to the p-side. This reduces the built-in potential barrier, allowing majority carriers to cross freely — exponential current flows. The diode equation is: I = I₀(e^(V/V_T) − 1), where V_T = kT/q ≈ 26 mV at room temperature and I₀ is the tiny reverse saturation current (~nA). In reverse bias, the barrier widens, majority carriers are pushed away from the junction, and only a negligible leakage current flows — the diode blocks. A diode thus acts as a one-way valve for current.

The practical knee voltage (where significant forward current begins) is ~0.6–0.7 V for silicon and ~0.2–0.3 V for germanium. A half-wave rectifier uses a single diode to pass only positive half-cycles of AC; a full-wave bridge rectifier uses four diodes arranged in a bridge to convert both half-cycles to the same polarity. Rectification is the first step in every DC power supply. Special diodes include Zener diodes (designed to conduct in reverse at a precise breakdown voltage — used for voltage regulation), LEDs (light-emitting), and photodiodes (light-detecting).
