Laws of Thermodynamics

The foundational principles governing energy, heat, work, and the direction of natural processes.

Theory

The Zeroth Law establishes the concept of temperature and thermal equilibrium. The First Law of Thermodynamics is the conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems: ΔU = Q − W, where ΔU is the change in internal energy, Q is heat added to the system, and W is work done by the system. For an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature. Work done by a gas during expansion is W = ∫P dV, which equals the area under the curve on a PV diagram.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics can be stated several equivalent ways: heat spontaneously flows from hot to cold (never the reverse); no heat engine can be 100% efficient; the entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Entropy (S) is a measure of disorder or the number of microscopic configurations corresponding to a macroscopic state: ΔS = Q/T for a reversible process. Every real (irreversible) process increases the total entropy of the universe. This gives time its directionality — the "arrow of time."

The Third Law of Thermodynamics states that as temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero. It is impossible to reach absolute zero in a finite number of steps. On PV diagrams, common thermodynamic processes include: isothermal (constant T, hyperbolic curve), isobaric (constant P, horizontal line), isochoric (constant V, vertical line), and adiabatic (no heat exchange, steeper than isothermal, following PVᵞ = constant where γ is the heat capacity ratio). The area enclosed by a cycle on a PV diagram equals the net work done per cycle.
