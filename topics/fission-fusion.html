Nuclear Fission & Fusion

Splitting heavy atoms and combining light atoms — the most powerful energy sources in the universe.

Theory

The binding energy of a nucleus is the energy required to disassemble it into individual protons and neutrons. The binding energy per nucleon varies across the periodic table, peaking at iron-56. This curve explains both fission and fusion: energy is released when nuclei move toward the peak. Nuclear fission splits a heavy nucleus (like uranium-235) into two medium-sized fragments, releasing energy because the products are more tightly bound. A neutron striking U-235 causes it to split, releasing about 200 MeV per fission, along with 2–3 additional neutrons: ΔE = Δmc².

These released neutrons can trigger further fissions, creating a chain reaction. In a nuclear reactor, control rods absorb excess neutrons to maintain a steady, controlled chain reaction. In a nuclear weapon, the chain reaction is uncontrolled and explosive. The critical mass is the minimum amount of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction. Nuclear power plants produce about 10% of the world's electricity with no direct carbon emissions, though they generate radioactive waste that must be stored safely for thousands of years.

Nuclear fusion combines light nuclei (typically hydrogen isotopes) to form heavier ones, releasing even more energy per unit mass than fission. The Sun and all stars are powered by fusion: in the Sun's core, hydrogen fuses into helium at temperatures of about 15 million kelvins via the proton-proton chain. On Earth, achieving controlled fusion is extremely difficult because nuclei must overcome their electrostatic repulsion (the Coulomb barrier), requiring temperatures exceeding 100 million kelvins. Experimental reactors like ITER use magnetic confinement of plasma. If achieved, fusion would provide virtually limitless clean energy from abundant hydrogen fuel.
