Free-Body Diagrams

A visual tool for identifying and analyzing all forces acting on an object.

Theory

A free-body diagram (FBD) is a simplified drawing that shows a single object isolated from its surroundings with all external forces acting on it represented as arrows. Drawing FBDs is the most important problem-solving skill in Newtonian mechanics. To create one: (1) identify the object of interest, (2) draw it as a simple shape (usually a dot or box), (3) identify every force acting on it — gravity, normal forces, tension, friction, applied forces, etc. — and (4) draw each force as an arrow starting from the object, pointing in the correct direction with a length proportional to its magnitude.

Common forces you will encounter include: weight (W = mg, always directed downward), the normal force (perpendicular to the contact surface), tension (along a rope or string, pulling away from the object), friction (parallel to the surface, opposing relative motion, with static friction up to f_s ≤ μ_s N and kinetic friction f_k = μ_k N), and applied forces (pushes or pulls from external agents).

Once the FBD is drawn, choose a coordinate system (often aligning one axis with the direction of acceleration), resolve all forces into components, and apply Newton's Second Law: ΣF_x = ma_x and ΣF_y = ma_y. For objects in equilibrium, both sums equal zero. For inclined planes, it is usually best to tilt the coordinate axes so that x is along the plane and y is perpendicular to it. Mastering FBDs transforms intimidating physics problems into systematic, solvable exercises.
