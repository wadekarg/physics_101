The Standard Model

The comprehensive theory classifying all known elementary particles and three of the four fundamental forces.

Theory

The Standard Model of particle physics is our best theory of the fundamental building blocks of matter and their interactions. It classifies all known elementary particles into two groups: fermions (matter particles, with half-integer spin) and bosons (force-carrying particles, with integer spin). Fermions are further divided into quarks (which make up protons, neutrons, and other hadrons) and leptons (including electrons, muons, taus, and their associated neutrinos). There are six quarks: up, down, charm, strange, top, and bottom.

The four fundamental forces are mediated by bosons. The electromagnetic force is carried by photons and acts on electrically charged particles. The strong nuclear force is carried by gluons and binds quarks into protons and neutrons, and protons and neutrons into nuclei. The weak nuclear force is carried by W and Z bosons and is responsible for beta decay and some types of nuclear reactions. The Higgs boson, discovered at CERN in 2012, is associated with the Higgs field that gives particles their mass. The fourth force, gravity, is not yet incorporated into the Standard Model.

Protons consist of two up quarks and one down quark (uud), while neutrons are two down quarks and one up quark (udd). Quarks carry fractional electric charge (+2/3 for up-type, −1/3 for down-type) and come in three "colors" (the charge of the strong force). Quarks are never found in isolation due to color confinement — they are always bound into color-neutral combinations. The Standard Model has been extraordinarily successful, predicting the existence of particles (like the top quark and Higgs boson) before they were discovered. However, it does not explain dark matter, dark energy, gravity at the quantum level, or why neutrinos have mass, suggesting a more complete theory remains to be found.
