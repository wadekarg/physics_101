Units & Measurement

How physicists quantify the natural world using standardized units and careful measurement techniques.

Theory

Physics is a quantitative science — every measurement requires a number and a unit. The internationally agreed system of units is the SI system (Système International), which defines seven base units: the meter (m) for length, the kilogram (kg) for mass, the second (s) for time, the ampere (A) for electric current, the kelvin (K) for temperature, the mole (mol) for amount of substance, and the candela (cd) for luminous intensity. All other units in physics are derived from these base units. For example, force is measured in newtons: 1 N = 1 kg · m/s².

Dimensional analysis is a powerful technique for checking equations and converting units. Every valid physics equation must be dimensionally consistent — the dimensions on the left side must match the dimensions on the right. For instance, kinetic energy KE = ½mv² has dimensions of [M][L²][T⁻²], which matches the SI unit of joules. Dimensional analysis can also reveal errors: if you derive an equation and find the dimensions don't match, something has gone wrong. Prefixes like kilo (10³), mega (10⁶), milli (10⁻³), and micro (10⁻⁶) are used to express very large or very small quantities conveniently.

Every measurement has an associated uncertainty, and understanding significant figures helps communicate this precision. The number of significant figures in a measurement reflects how precisely it was made. For example, 3.14 has three significant figures, while 3.1400 has five. When performing calculations, the result should be rounded to match the least precise input: for multiplication and division, match the fewest significant figures; for addition and subtraction, match the fewest decimal places. Understanding uncertainty and significant figures is essential for producing trustworthy experimental results.
