Orbital Mechanics

The physics governing satellites, planets, and spacecraft in gravitational orbits.

Theory

Orbital mechanics applies Newton's gravitation and laws of motion to objects in orbit. For a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the centripetal force: GMm/r² = mv²/r, giving the orbital velocity v = √(GM/r). Remarkably, the orbital speed depends only on the mass of the central body and the orbital radius, not on the mass of the orbiting object. This is why all objects in the same orbit move at the same speed, and why astronauts in orbit experience "weightlessness" — they are in free fall together with their spacecraft.

Kepler's three laws describe orbital motion: (1) orbits are ellipses with the central body at one focus; (2) a line from the planet to the star sweeps equal areas in equal times (conservation of angular momentum); (3) the square of the period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis: T² = (4π²/GM)a³. For circular orbits, a equals the radius r, and the period is T = 2πr/v = 2π√(r³/GM).

A geostationary orbit has a period of exactly 24 hours, so the satellite stays above the same point on Earth's equator. Using Kepler's Third Law, this requires an altitude of about 35,786 km. Satellites in lower orbits move faster and have shorter periods. Changing orbits requires thrust — the Hohmann transfer orbit is the most fuel-efficient way to move between two circular orbits. Understanding orbital mechanics is essential for satellite communications, GPS, space exploration, and understanding the dynamics of the solar system.
