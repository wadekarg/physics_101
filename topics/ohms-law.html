Ohm's Law & Resistance

The fundamental relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in electrical circuits.

Theory

Ohm's Law states that the voltage across a conductor is proportional to the current through it: V = IR, where V is voltage (volts), I is current (amperes), and R is resistance (ohms, Ω). Current is the rate of flow of electric charge: I = ΔQ/Δt. Conventional current flows from high to low potential (positive to negative), though electrons actually move in the opposite direction. A current of 1 ampere means 1 coulomb of charge passes a point each second.

Resistance opposes the flow of current and converts electrical energy to heat. The resistance of a wire depends on its material, length, and cross-sectional area: R = ρL/A, where ρ is the resistivity of the material (in Ω·m). Good conductors like copper have low resistivity; insulators like rubber have extremely high resistivity. Resistivity typically increases with temperature for metals. Resistor color codes use colored bands to indicate resistance values: each color represents a digit (black=0, brown=1, red=2, ..., white=9), with bands for digits and a multiplier.

The power dissipated by a resistor is P = IV = I²R = V²/R. This is why high-resistance heating elements get hot and why power transmission uses high voltage (to reduce current and thus reduce I²R losses in the wires). Ohm's Law applies to ohmic materials where R is constant regardless of voltage. Non-ohmic devices (like diodes and LEDs) have resistance that varies with voltage. Despite this, Ohm's Law remains the cornerstone of circuit analysis and is used every day by electrical engineers worldwide.
