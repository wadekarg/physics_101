Newton's First Law — Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by a net force.

Theory

Newton's First Law, often called the Law of Inertia, states that an object will remain at rest or continue moving in a straight line at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force. This was revolutionary because it contradicted the ancient Aristotelian view that objects naturally tend to come to rest. In reality, objects slow down due to friction and air resistance — forces that are always present in everyday experience but are not fundamental to motion itself.

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia: a more massive object is harder to accelerate, decelerate, or change direction. When the net force on an object is zero (ΣF = 0), the object is in equilibrium. This can mean it is at rest (static equilibrium) or moving at constant velocity (dynamic equilibrium). A book on a table is in static equilibrium; a car cruising at constant speed on a straight highway is in dynamic equilibrium.

The First Law also implicitly defines inertial reference frames — frames of reference in which Newton's laws hold true. An inertial frame is one that is not accelerating. If you are in an accelerating car, you feel "pushed" backward — but this is a fictitious force arising because the car is a non-inertial frame. Newton's First Law is the foundation upon which all of classical mechanics is built, and understanding it deeply is essential before moving to the Second and Third Laws.
