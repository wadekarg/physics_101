Vectors

Mathematical quantities with both magnitude and direction that form the language of physics.

Theory

In physics, quantities fall into two categories: scalars (described by magnitude alone, like mass or temperature) and vectors (described by both magnitude and direction, like velocity or force). A vector is typically represented as an arrow whose length indicates its magnitude and whose orientation indicates its direction. In two dimensions, any vector can be broken into components along the x and y axes: Aₓ = A cos θ and Aᵧ = A sin θ, where A is the magnitude and θ is the angle measured from the positive x-axis.

Vectors are added using the tip-to-tail method or, equivalently, by adding their components: R⃗ = A⃗ + B⃗ means Rₓ = Aₓ + Bₓ and Rᵧ = Aᵧ + Bᵧ. The magnitude of the resultant is R = √(Rₓ² + Rᵧ²), and its direction is θ = tan⁻¹(Rᵧ / Rₓ). Subtracting vectors is equivalent to adding the negative: A⃗ − B⃗ = A⃗ + (−B⃗), where −B⃗ has the same magnitude as B⃗ but points in the opposite direction.

Two important vector operations arise frequently in physics. The dot product (scalar product) is defined as A⃗ · B⃗ = AB cos θ, where θ is the angle between the two vectors. It yields a scalar and is used in calculating work (W = F⃗ · d⃗). The cross product (vector product) gives a new vector perpendicular to both inputs, with magnitude |A⃗ × B⃗| = AB sin θ. The cross product is essential for torque (τ⃗ = r⃗ × F⃗) and magnetic force calculations. Mastering vectors is the foundation for nearly every topic in physics.
