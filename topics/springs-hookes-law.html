Springs & Hooke's Law

The restoring force of springs and the elegant motion known as simple harmonic motion.

Theory

Hooke's Law states that the restoring force exerted by a spring is proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and directed opposite to the displacement: F = −kx, where k is the spring constant (in N/m) and x is the displacement. A stiffer spring has a larger k. This linear restoring force produces simple harmonic motion (SHM) — the mass oscillates back and forth with a sinusoidal pattern: x(t) = A cos(ωt + φ), where A is the amplitude, ω is the angular frequency, and φ is the phase constant.

The period of a mass on a spring is T = 2π√(m/k), and the frequency is f = (1/2π)√(k/m). Notably, the period does not depend on the amplitude — large and small oscillations take the same time. During SHM, energy continuously converts between kinetic and potential forms: at the equilibrium position, all energy is kinetic; at maximum displacement, all energy is potential: E = ½kA² = ½mv_max². The maximum velocity occurs at equilibrium: v_max = Aω.

Hooke's Law applies to many systems beyond literal metal springs: elastic bands, bending beams, vibrating molecules, and even the small oscillations of any system near a stable equilibrium point. When the displacement becomes too large, most real springs deviate from Hooke's Law, and the motion is no longer perfectly harmonic. The elastic potential energy stored in a spring is PE = ½kx², a parabolic function that is characteristic of all SHM systems. Understanding springs and SHM provides the foundation for studying waves, sound, and quantum mechanics.
