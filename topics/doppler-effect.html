Doppler Effect & Sonic Booms

The change in observed frequency when a wave source and observer are in relative motion.

Theory

The Doppler effect is the change in observed frequency (and wavelength) of a wave when the source and observer are moving relative to each other. When a source approaches, the observed frequency is higher (higher pitch for sound, bluer color for light); when it recedes, the frequency is lower (lower pitch, redder color). For sound, the general formula is: f' = f(v ± v_o)/(v ∓ v_s), where v is the wave speed, v_o is the observer speed, and v_s is the source speed. The upper signs apply when source and observer approach each other.

The Doppler effect has countless applications. Police radar guns use the Doppler shift of reflected microwaves to measure vehicle speeds. Medical ultrasound uses the Doppler effect to measure blood flow velocity. In astronomy, the redshift of light from distant galaxies reveals that the universe is expanding — one of the most important discoveries in modern science. Weather radar uses the Doppler shift to detect wind patterns and rotation in storms.

When a source moves faster than the speed of sound, it creates a shock wave known as a sonic boom. The wave fronts pile up into a cone-shaped wavefront called a Mach cone. The half-angle of this cone is given by sin θ = v/v_s = 1/M, where M is the Mach number (the ratio of object speed to sound speed). A Mach number greater than 1 means supersonic flight. The sonic boom is not a one-time event — it is a continuous shock wave that follows the aircraft. The loud "boom" is heard when the cone sweeps past the observer.
