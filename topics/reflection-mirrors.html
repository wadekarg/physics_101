Reflection & Mirrors

How light bounces off surfaces and how mirrors form images.

Theory

The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection: θ_i = θ_r, where both angles are measured from the normal (the line perpendicular to the surface). Specular reflection occurs on smooth surfaces (mirrors) where all reflected rays are parallel, forming clear images. Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces where reflected rays scatter in many directions, allowing us to see objects from any angle. Both types obey the law of reflection at each microscopic surface element.

A flat (plane) mirror forms a virtual image that appears to be behind the mirror, at the same distance as the object is in front. The image is upright, the same size, but laterally inverted (left-right reversed). Curved mirrors can be concave (converging) or convex (diverging). The mirror equation relates object distance (d_o), image distance (d_i), and focal length (f): 1/d_o + 1/d_i = 1/f. The magnification is m = −d_i/d_o. For a concave mirror, f = R/2, where R is the radius of curvature.

Concave mirrors can form both real images (formed where reflected rays actually converge, can be projected on a screen) and virtual images (formed where rays appear to diverge from, behind the mirror). When the object is beyond the focal point, a concave mirror forms a real, inverted image. When the object is inside the focal point, it forms a virtual, upright, magnified image (like a makeup mirror). Convex mirrors always form virtual, upright, diminished images and are used for wide-angle viewing (car side mirrors, security mirrors). Ray diagrams using three principal rays are the key tool for understanding image formation.
