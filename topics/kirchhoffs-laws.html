Kirchhoff's Laws & RC Circuits

Powerful rules for analyzing complex circuits and the time-dependent behavior of capacitor circuits.

Theory

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) states that the total current entering any junction equals the total current leaving: ΣI_in = ΣI_out. This is a consequence of conservation of charge. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) states that the sum of all voltage changes around any closed loop is zero: ΣV = 0. This is a consequence of conservation of energy. Together, these two laws allow you to analyze any circuit, no matter how complex, by writing equations for each junction and loop.

To apply Kirchhoff's Laws: (1) label all currents with assumed directions, (2) apply KCL at each junction, (3) apply KVL around each independent loop (voltage rises for batteries from − to + terminal, voltage drops across resistors in the direction of current), and (4) solve the resulting system of equations. If a current comes out negative, it simply means the actual direction is opposite to your assumed direction.

An RC circuit contains a resistor and capacitor. When charging, the voltage across the capacitor grows exponentially: V_C(t) = V₀(1 − e^(−t/RC)). When discharging: V_C(t) = V₀ e^(−t/RC). The product τ = RC is the time constant — the time for the voltage to reach about 63% of its final value (charging) or drop to about 37% (discharging). After 5 time constants, the capacitor is essentially fully charged or discharged. RC circuits are fundamental to timing circuits, filters, and signal processing in electronics.
