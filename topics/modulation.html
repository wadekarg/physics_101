Modulation & Signal Transmission

How low-frequency audio signals are encoded onto high-frequency carrier waves for long-distance transmission.

Theory

A microphone converts sound into an electrical message signal at audio frequencies (20 Hz–20 kHz). To transmit it through the air as radio waves, a simple antenna would need to be kilometres long (antenna length ≈ λ/4, so at 10 kHz the antenna must be 7.5 km long!). Modulation solves this by combining the message with a high-frequency carrier wave (hundreds of kHz to GHz), shrinking the required antenna to a practical size.

In Amplitude Modulation (AM), the carrier's amplitude is varied in proportion to the message: s(t) = A_c[1 + μ·sin(2πf_m·t)]sin(2πf_c·t). The modulation index μ = A_m/A_c must be ≤ 1 to avoid over-modulation and distortion. AM produces two sidebands at f_c ± f_m, so the total bandwidth = 2f_m. AM is simple and cheap but susceptible to noise (which corrupts amplitude). In Frequency Modulation (FM), the carrier's frequency is varied: s(t) = A_c·sin(2πf_c·t + β·sin(2πf_m·t)). The modulation index β = Δf/f_m gives FM a much wider bandwidth but far better noise immunity.

The radio spectrum is divided into bands: AM broadcast uses Medium Wave (MW, 530–1710 kHz); FM broadcast uses 88–108 MHz. Television uses VHF/UHF bands. Mobile phones use GHz bands. All modern digital communication (4G, 5G, WiFi) uses Quadrature Amplitude Modulation (QAM) — a sophisticated form that simultaneously modulates both amplitude and phase to pack more bits per hertz. The transition from analogue to digital broadcasting (like India's FM phase and DD Free Dish) uses this principle.
