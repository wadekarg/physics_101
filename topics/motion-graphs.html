Motion Graphs

Visual representations that reveal the relationships between position, velocity, and acceleration over time.

Theory

Motion graphs provide a powerful visual way to understand and analyze movement. The three main types are position-time (x-t), velocity-time (v-t), and acceleration-time (a-t) graphs. On a position-time graph, the slope at any point equals the instantaneous velocity: a steep slope means high speed, a flat line means the object is at rest, and a negative slope means the object is moving in the negative direction. A straight line on a position-time graph indicates constant velocity, while a curve indicates changing velocity (acceleration).

On a velocity-time graph, the slope equals the acceleration. A horizontal line means constant velocity (zero acceleration), while a sloped line means constant acceleration. The crucial insight is that the area under the velocity-time curve equals the displacement: Δx = ∫v dt. Similarly, on an acceleration-time graph, the area under the curve gives the change in velocity: Δv = ∫a dt. These graphical relationships mirror the calculus connections between position, velocity, and acceleration.

Being able to translate between different types of motion graphs is a key skill. For example, constant acceleration appears as a parabola on a position-time graph, a straight line on a velocity-time graph, and a horizontal line on an acceleration-time graph. If an object reverses direction, the velocity-time graph crosses zero, and the position-time graph has a turning point. Practicing with motion graphs builds deep intuition about how objects move and is an essential step before tackling more complex problems in mechanics.
