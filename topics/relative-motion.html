Relative Motion

How the observed motion of an object depends on the reference frame of the observer.

Theory

Relative motion describes how the velocity of an object differs depending on the observer's reference frame. A reference frame is simply a coordinate system attached to an observer. If you stand on the ground and watch a train pass at 30 m/s, the train's velocity relative to you is 30 m/s. But a passenger sitting inside the train sees you moving backward at 30 m/s, while they perceive themselves as stationary. Both descriptions are equally valid.

The key equation for relative velocity in one dimension is v_AC = v_AB + v_BC, where v_AC is the velocity of A relative to C, v_AB is the velocity of A relative to B, and v_BC is the velocity of B relative to C. In two dimensions, this becomes a vector equation: ⃗v_AC = ⃗v_AB + ⃗v_BC. A classic example is a boat crossing a river: the boat's velocity relative to the ground is the vector sum of its velocity relative to the water and the water's velocity relative to the ground.

Relative motion is central to classical physics and becomes even more interesting in Einstein's special relativity, where the simple velocity addition formula must be modified at speeds approaching the speed of light. At everyday speeds, however, the Galilean velocity addition works perfectly. Understanding reference frames helps solve problems involving airplanes in wind, boats in currents, and any situation where multiple objects are in motion simultaneously.
