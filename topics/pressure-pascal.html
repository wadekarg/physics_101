Pressure & Pascal's Principle

How force distributed over area creates pressure and how it transmits through confined fluids.

Theory

Pressure is defined as force per unit area: P = F/A, measured in pascals (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m². Atmospheric pressure at sea level is approximately 1 atm = 101,325 Pa. In a fluid at rest, pressure increases with depth due to the weight of the fluid above: P = P₀ + ρgh, where ρ is the fluid density, g is gravitational acceleration, and h is the depth below the surface. This explains why your ears feel pressure when diving — every 10 meters of water adds roughly one atmosphere of pressure.

Pascal's Principle states that a change in pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of the container. This principle is the basis of hydraulic systems. In a hydraulic press, a small force applied to a small piston creates the same pressure as a large force on a large piston: F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂. This means the output force can be much larger than the input force, providing a mechanical advantage of A₂/A₁. Hydraulic brakes, car lifts, and heavy machinery all exploit this principle.

Pressure in fluids is measured using various devices. A manometer uses a column of liquid to measure pressure differences. A barometer measures atmospheric pressure using a mercury column (standard atmospheric pressure supports a column of about 760 mm Hg). Gauge pressure is the pressure above atmospheric: P_gauge = P_absolute − P_atm. Tire pressure gauges, for example, read gauge pressure. Understanding pressure is essential for engineering, medicine (blood pressure), meteorology, and any application involving fluids.
