Standing Waves & Harmonics

Wave patterns that appear stationary, forming the basis of musical sound and resonance.

Theory

A standing wave is formed when two identical waves traveling in opposite directions interfere with each other. Unlike traveling waves, standing waves do not transport energy — instead, they oscillate in place. The points of zero displacement are called nodes, and the points of maximum displacement are called antinodes. Standing waves commonly form when a wave reflects back and forth in a confined medium, such as a vibrating guitar string or air in a pipe.

For a string fixed at both ends (like a guitar string), standing waves can only form at specific frequencies called harmonics. The fundamental frequency (first harmonic) has wavelength λ₁ = 2L and frequency f₁ = v/(2L), where L is the string length and v is the wave speed. Higher harmonics have frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental: f_n = nf₁ (n = 1, 2, 3, ...). Each harmonic has n antinodes and n+1 nodes (including the fixed ends).

Standing waves also form in open and closed pipes. An open pipe (open at both ends) supports all harmonics, like a string. A closed pipe (closed at one end) only supports odd harmonics: f_n = nv/(4L) (n = 1, 3, 5, ...) because there must be a node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end. The specific mix of harmonics (overtones) present in a sound determines its timbre or tone quality — this is why a trumpet and a violin playing the same note sound different. Standing waves and harmonics are central to understanding music, acoustics, and even quantum mechanics (electron standing waves in atoms).
