Bernoulli's Equation

The relationship between fluid speed, pressure, and height in flowing fluids.

Theory

Bernoulli's Equation describes the behavior of an ideal (incompressible, non-viscous) fluid flowing steadily along a streamline: P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant. This equation is essentially conservation of energy applied to fluid flow. The three terms represent pressure energy, kinetic energy per unit volume, and gravitational potential energy per unit volume, respectively. Where the fluid speeds up, the pressure drops — and vice versa.

The continuity equation, A₁v₁ = A₂v₂, states that the volume flow rate is constant in an incompressible fluid flowing through a pipe of varying cross-section. Where the pipe narrows, the fluid speeds up. Combining continuity with Bernoulli's equation explains many phenomena. For example, when water flows through a constriction in a pipe, the velocity increases and the pressure decreases. This is the Venturi effect, used in carburetors, atomizers, and flow meters.

Bernoulli's principle helps explain (in simplified form) how airplane wings generate lift: air flows faster over the curved top surface, reducing pressure relative to the flatter bottom surface, creating an upward net force. It also explains why shower curtains get pulled inward (fast-flowing water lowers the air pressure inside), why two ships passing close together are drawn toward each other, and how a curveball in baseball curves. However, real fluids have viscosity and turbulence, so Bernoulli's equation is an idealization — more complete models include corrections for these effects.
