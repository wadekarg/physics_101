Radioactive Decay

The spontaneous transformation of unstable atomic nuclei and the probabilistic nature of decay.

Theory

Radioactive decay is the spontaneous emission of particles or energy from unstable atomic nuclei. There are three main types. Alpha decay (α): the nucleus emits a helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons), reducing the atomic number by 2 and mass number by 4. Beta decay (β): a neutron converts to a proton (or vice versa), emitting an electron (or positron) and a neutrino. Gamma decay (γ): the nucleus releases a high-energy photon without changing its composition, typically after an alpha or beta decay leaves it in an excited state.

Radioactive decay is a random, probabilistic process. You cannot predict when a particular atom will decay, but for a large collection of atoms, the decay rate is predictable. The number of atoms remaining after time t is N = N₀(1/2)^(t/τ), where N₀ is the initial number and τ is the half-life — the time for half the atoms to decay. Half-lives range from fractions of a second (for highly unstable isotopes) to billions of years (uranium-238: 4.5 billion years). The activity (decays per second) is A = λN, where λ = ln2/τ is the decay constant.

Radioactivity has numerous applications. Carbon-14 dating uses the known half-life of ¹⁴C (5730 years) to determine the age of organic material up to about 50,000 years old. Medical imaging uses radioactive tracers (PET scans use positron emitters). Radiation therapy targets cancer cells with focused gamma rays or particle beams. Smoke detectors use americium-241 alpha emitters. Nuclear power harnesses the energy from controlled fission of uranium. Understanding radioactive decay is also essential for nuclear safety, waste management, and environmental monitoring.
