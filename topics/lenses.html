Lenses & Image Formation

How curved transparent elements bend light to form images in cameras, eyes, and microscopes.

Theory

A lens is a transparent object that refracts light to form images. Converging (convex) lenses are thicker in the middle and bring parallel rays to a focus at the focal point. Diverging (concave) lenses are thinner in the middle and cause parallel rays to spread as if coming from a virtual focal point. The thin lens equation is identical in form to the mirror equation: 1/d_o + 1/d_i = 1/f, where f is positive for converging lenses and negative for diverging lenses.

The magnification is m = −d_i/d_o = h_i/h_o, where h_i and h_o are the image and object heights. A converging lens can form real (inverted) or virtual (upright) images depending on the object position. When the object is beyond 2f, the image is real, inverted, and reduced. Between f and 2f, the image is real, inverted, and magnified. Inside f, the image is virtual, upright, and magnified (a magnifying glass). Diverging lenses always form virtual, upright, reduced images.

Lenses are fundamental to countless optical instruments. The human eye uses a flexible lens to focus images on the retina, with the cornea providing most of the refracting power. Cameras use lens systems to focus images on film or sensors. Microscopes combine two converging lenses to achieve high magnification. Telescopes use large objective lenses (or mirrors) to collect light from distant objects. The lensmaker's equation relates focal length to the lens curvatures and refractive index: 1/f = (n−1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂). Lens aberrations (chromatic, spherical) are corrected using combinations of lenses with different shapes and materials.
