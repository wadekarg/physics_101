Projectile Motion

Two-dimensional motion under gravity where horizontal and vertical components are independent.

Theory

Projectile motion is the motion of an object launched into the air that moves under the influence of gravity alone (ignoring air resistance). The key insight is that the horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent of each other. Horizontally, the object moves with constant velocity: x = v₀ cos θ · t. Vertically, the object undergoes free fall with acceleration g downward: y = v₀ sin θ · t − ½gt². These two components combine to produce the characteristic parabolic trajectory.

Several useful results follow from this analysis. The time of flight (for launch and landing at the same height) is T = 2v₀ sin θ / g. The maximum height reached is H = v₀² sin²θ / (2g). The range (horizontal distance) is R = v₀² sin 2θ / g. From the range equation, we can see that the maximum range is achieved at a launch angle of 45 degrees, and that complementary angles (like 30 and 60 degrees) give the same range but different trajectories.

In real-world situations, air resistance modifies these ideal results. Drag reduces the range significantly, the trajectory is no longer a perfect parabola, and the optimal launch angle is less than 45 degrees. Nonetheless, the idealized model provides an excellent first approximation and illustrates the powerful technique of decomposing motion into independent perpendicular components — a method that will be used throughout physics.
