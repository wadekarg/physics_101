Circular Motion

Understanding the physics of objects moving along curved paths at constant or varying speeds.

Theory

When an object moves in a circle at constant speed, it is undergoing uniform circular motion. Even though the speed is constant, the velocity is continuously changing direction, which means the object is accelerating. This acceleration is directed toward the center of the circle and is called centripetal acceleration: a_c = v²/r, where v is the speed and r is the radius. The force that causes this acceleration is the centripetal force: F_c = mv²/r. This is not a new type of force — it is whatever force (gravity, tension, friction, normal force) keeps the object on its circular path.

Circular motion is described by angular quantities. The period T is the time for one complete revolution, and the frequency f is the number of revolutions per second: f = 1/T. The angular velocity is ω = 2π/T = 2πf, measured in radians per second. The linear speed relates to angular velocity by v = ωr. These relationships allow us to convert between the linear and angular descriptions of circular motion.

If an object moves in a circle with changing speed, it has both centripetal acceleration (directed inward) and tangential acceleration (directed along the velocity). The total acceleration is the vector sum of these two components. Examples of circular motion are everywhere: planets orbiting the Sun, cars rounding curves, electrons in magnetic fields, and clothes in a spinning dryer. Understanding centripetal acceleration is essential for analyzing any curved-path motion in physics.
