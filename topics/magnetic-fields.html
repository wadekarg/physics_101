Magnetic Fields & Forces

The nature of magnetic fields, how they are produced, and their fundamental properties.

Theory

A magnetic field (⃗B) is a vector field produced by moving charges (currents) and magnetic materials. The SI unit is the tesla (T), where 1 T = 1 kg/(A·s²). Magnetic field lines form closed loops — they emerge from the north pole of a magnet and enter the south pole, continuing through the interior. Unlike electric field lines, magnetic field lines never begin or end at a point, reflecting the fact that magnetic monopoles have never been observed: ∮⃗B · d⃗A = 0 (Gauss's law for magnetism).

A current-carrying wire creates a magnetic field. For a long straight wire, the field circles the wire with magnitude B = μ₀I/(2πr), where μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A is the permeability of free space. The direction is given by the right-hand rule: point your thumb in the direction of current, and your fingers curl in the direction of the magnetic field. Multiple wires create fields that superpose as vectors.

Earth has its own magnetic field, approximately that of a dipole tilted about 11° from the rotation axis. The field strength at the surface is roughly 25–65 μT. Earth's magnetic field protects life by deflecting charged particles from the solar wind, channeling them toward the poles where they create the aurora borealis and australis. The field is generated by convection currents in Earth's liquid iron outer core (the geodynamo). Earth's magnetic poles have reversed hundreds of times in geological history, a fact recorded in the magnetization of volcanic rocks on the seafloor.
