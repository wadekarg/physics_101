Power & Efficiency

The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred, and how efficiently machines operate.

Theory

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred: P = W/t, where W is work and t is time. The SI unit of power is the watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s. For a constant force moving an object at constant velocity, power can also be expressed as P = Fv cos θ. A more powerful engine can do the same amount of work in less time, or more work in the same time. Common power units include kilowatts (kW) and horsepower (hp), where 1 hp ≈ 746 W.

The concept of power is essential for understanding machines, engines, and electrical devices. A 100 W light bulb converts 100 joules of electrical energy per second into light and heat. A 2000 W microwave transfers 2000 joules per second to your food. The kilowatt-hour (kWh) is a unit of energy (not power) used by electric companies: 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J. Understanding the distinction between energy and power is crucial for analyzing any real-world system.

Efficiency measures how well a machine converts input energy into useful output energy: η = useful output energy / total input energy × 100%, or equivalently η = P_out / P_in × 100%. No real machine is 100% efficient — some energy is always "lost" to friction, heat, sound, or other non-useful forms. An incandescent bulb is about 5% efficient (most energy becomes heat), while LED bulbs reach about 40%. Electric motors can achieve 90%+ efficiency. Improving efficiency is one of the most important goals in engineering and environmental science.
