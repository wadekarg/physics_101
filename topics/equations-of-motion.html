Equations of Motion

The kinematic equations that relate position, velocity, acceleration, and time for constant acceleration.

Theory

When an object moves with constant acceleration, its motion can be completely described by a set of kinematic equations. These five equations relate the quantities: initial velocity (v₀), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), time (t), and displacement (Δx). The most commonly used forms are: v = v₀ + at, Δx = v₀t + ½at², v² = v₀² + 2aΔx, Δx = ½(v₀ + v)t, and Δx = vt − ½at².

Each equation is useful in different scenarios depending on which quantities are known and which are unknown. For example, if you know the initial velocity and acceleration but not the final velocity, you might use Δx = v₀t + ½at² to find displacement. The key strategy is to identify the three known quantities and the one unknown, then select the equation that contains exactly those four variables. These equations are derived by integrating the constant-acceleration assumption: since a = dv/dt is constant, integrating once gives velocity as a linear function of time, and integrating again gives position as a quadratic function of time.

It is crucial to remember that these equations are only valid for constant (uniform) acceleration. In situations where acceleration varies — such as a car that gradually increases its throttle — these simple equations do not apply, and calculus-based methods must be used. Also, be careful with signs: choose a positive direction (e.g., upward or rightward) and consistently apply it to all vector quantities. A negative acceleration does not always mean "slowing down" — it means the acceleration points in the negative direction.
