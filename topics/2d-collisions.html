2D Collisions & Center of Mass

Analyzing collisions in two dimensions and understanding the center of mass of a system.

Theory

In two-dimensional collisions, momentum is conserved independently in each direction: Σp_x,i = Σp_x,f and Σp_y,i = Σp_y,f. This gives us two equations, which means we need additional information (such as the angle of deflection or whether the collision is elastic) to fully solve the problem. A common example is a billiard ball striking another at an angle: the two balls fly off in different directions, but the vector sum of their momenta equals the initial momentum.

The center of mass (COM) of a system is the mass-weighted average position: x_cm = Σ(m_i x_i) / Σm_i. The center of mass moves as if all the mass were concentrated there and all external forces acted on that point. For an isolated system, the velocity of the center of mass is constant: v_cm = p_total / M_total. In any collision (elastic or inelastic), the center of mass continues moving at the same velocity throughout.

Analyzing collisions in the center-of-mass frame simplifies many problems. In this frame, the total momentum is zero, so the two objects approach each other and recede symmetrically. For elastic collisions in the COM frame, each object simply reverses its velocity. The center of mass concept extends beyond collisions — it explains why a thrown wrench rotates around its center of mass, and why the center of mass of a system follows a smooth parabolic path even if individual parts tumble and spin.
