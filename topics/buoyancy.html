Buoyancy & Archimedes

Why objects float or sink, explained by the upward force fluids exert on submerged objects.

Theory

Archimedes' Principle states that any object submerged in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces: F_b = ρ_fluid · g · V_displaced. This force arises because fluid pressure increases with depth, so the pressure pushing up on the bottom of an object is greater than the pressure pushing down on the top. The buoyant force acts at the center of the displaced volume (the center of buoyancy).

Whether an object floats or sinks depends on the comparison between its weight and the buoyant force, which reduces to comparing densities. If the object's average density is less than the fluid's density (ρ_object < ρ_fluid), it floats. If greater, it sinks. A floating object displaces exactly its own weight of fluid. For example, ice has a density of about 917 kg/m³, less than water's 1000 kg/m³, so ice floats with about 91.7% of its volume submerged. This is why icebergs are mostly underwater.

Archimedes' Principle applies to all fluids, including gases. A helium balloon floats in air because helium is less dense than the surrounding atmosphere. Ships made of steel float because their hull shape displaces a large volume of water, making the ship's average density (including the air inside) less than water. Submarines control their buoyancy by filling or emptying ballast tanks with water. The legend says Archimedes discovered this principle while taking a bath and ran through the streets shouting "Eureka!" — though the historical accuracy of this story is debated.
