Angular Momentum

The rotational analog of linear momentum and its powerful conservation law.

Theory

Angular momentum is the rotational analog of linear momentum. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis, angular momentum is L = Iω, where I is the moment of inertia and ω is the angular velocity. For a point particle, angular momentum about a point is L = mvr sin θ, or in vector form ⃗L = ⃗r × ⃗p. Angular momentum is measured in kg·m²/s and is a vector quantity, with direction given by the right-hand rule.

The conservation of angular momentum states that if no net external torque acts on a system, the total angular momentum remains constant: Στ_ext = 0 → L = constant. This means I_iω_i = I_fω_f. A figure skater pulling in their arms decreases their moment of inertia, so their angular velocity increases to conserve angular momentum — they spin faster. Similarly, a diver in a tuck position rotates faster than in a layout position.

Conservation of angular momentum has profound consequences across all scales of physics. It explains why planets closer to the Sun orbit faster (Kepler's Second Law), why neutron stars spin incredibly fast after a star collapses, and why a gyroscope resists changes to its orientation. Angular momentum conservation is one of the fundamental conservation laws of nature, arising from the rotational symmetry of physical laws (Noether's theorem).
