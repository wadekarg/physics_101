Work & Kinetic Energy

How forces transfer energy to objects through work and change their kinetic energy.

Theory

In physics, work has a precise definition: work is done when a force causes a displacement. For a constant force at an angle θ to the displacement, work is W = Fd cos θ. If the force is perpendicular to the displacement (like the normal force on a sliding object on a flat surface), no work is done. Work is a scalar quantity measured in joules (J), where 1 J = 1 N · m. Work can be positive (force in the direction of motion), negative (force opposing motion, like friction), or zero.

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion, defined as KE = ½mv². The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy: W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mv₀². This is a powerful result because it relates force and displacement directly to speed, often bypassing the need to find acceleration and use kinematics. For variable forces, work is calculated as an integral: W = ∫F · dx, which geometrically equals the area under the force-displacement curve.

The work-energy theorem is particularly useful for problems involving friction, inclines, and varying forces. For example, to find the speed of a block after sliding down a rough incline, you can calculate the work done by gravity (positive), the work done by friction (negative), and set the net work equal to the change in kinetic energy. Energy methods are often simpler than force-based approaches because energy is a scalar — no vector components are needed.
