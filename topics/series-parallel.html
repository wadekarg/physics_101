Series & Parallel Circuits

Two fundamental ways to connect circuit elements and how they affect current and voltage.

Theory

Circuit elements can be connected in two basic configurations. In a series circuit, components are connected end-to-end so the same current flows through each: I_total = I₁ = I₂ = I₃. The voltages across each component add up to the total: V_total = V₁ + V₂ + V₃. The equivalent resistance of resistors in series is simply their sum: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + .... If any component in a series circuit fails (opens), the entire circuit stops working.

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across the same two nodes so they all have the same voltage: V_total = V₁ = V₂ = V₃. The currents through each branch add up to the total: I_total = I₁ + I₂ + I₃. The equivalent resistance of parallel resistors is found from: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + .... For two resistors in parallel: R_eq = R₁R₂/(R₁ + R₂). The equivalent resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance.

Most real circuits are combination circuits containing both series and parallel elements. To analyze them, identify series and parallel groups, simplify step by step to find the total resistance, then work backward to find individual currents and voltages. Household wiring uses parallel circuits so that each appliance gets the full supply voltage and can be operated independently. Understanding series and parallel combinations is essential for analyzing any electrical circuit, from simple flashlights to complex electronic systems.
