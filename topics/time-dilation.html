Time Dilation

How time runs slower for objects moving at speeds approaching the speed of light.

Theory

Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity (1905) is built on two postulates: (1) the laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames, and (2) the speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of their motion or the motion of the source: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s. These seemingly simple postulates lead to revolutionary consequences, the most famous being time dilation — moving clocks run slower than stationary ones.

The time dilation formula is Δt = γΔt₀, where Δt₀ is the proper time (measured by a clock at rest relative to the events) and γ = 1/√(1 − v²/c²) is the Lorentz factor. At everyday speeds, γ ≈ 1 and the effect is negligible. But at 90% of c, γ ≈ 2.3, meaning a moving clock ticks 2.3 times slower. The classic thought experiment is the light clock: a pulse of light bouncing between two mirrors. For a stationary observer, the light in a moving clock travels a longer diagonal path, taking more time per tick.

The twin paradox is a famous illustration: one twin travels at high speed to a distant star and back, while the other stays on Earth. When they reunite, the traveling twin has aged less. This is not just theoretical — it has been confirmed experimentally with atomic clocks on aircraft and with muon decay. Muons created by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere should decay before reaching the ground, but time dilation means they "live" longer in Earth's frame and are detected at the surface. GPS satellites must account for both special and general relativistic time effects to maintain accuracy.
