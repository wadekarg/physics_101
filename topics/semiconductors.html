Semiconductors & Energy Bands

How adding tiny amounts of impurities to silicon creates the foundation of all modern electronics.

Theory

A semiconductor is a material whose electrical conductivity falls between a conductor and an insulator. In silicon, valence electrons are bound in covalent bonds at low temperatures, but some gain enough thermal energy to break free, leaving behind a hole — the absence of an electron that acts like a positive charge carrier. The energy needed to free an electron is the band gap: about 1.12 eV for silicon and 0.67 eV for germanium. Conductors have zero (or overlapping) band gaps; insulators have very large ones (5.5 eV for diamond).

Pure (intrinsic) silicon has equal numbers of electrons and holes. We can dramatically increase conductivity by doping — adding tiny amounts of impurity atoms. N-type doping uses pentavalent atoms (phosphorus, arsenic) which donate an extra free electron, making electrons the majority carriers. P-type doping uses trivalent atoms (boron, aluminium) which create extra holes, making holes the majority carriers. Even a doping level of 1 part per 10 million (1016 cm−3 in a 1022 cm−3 silicon lattice) changes conductivity by orders of magnitude.

The position of the Fermi level — the energy at which the probability of electron occupation is 50% — shifts toward the conduction band for n-type semiconductors and toward the valence band for p-type. LEDs exploit the band gap directly: when an electron drops from the conduction band to fill a hole in the valence band, it releases energy exactly equal to the band gap as a photon of light. By engineering semiconductors with different band gaps (using alloys like GaAs, GaN, InGaN), engineers produce LEDs of any wavelength from infrared to ultraviolet.
