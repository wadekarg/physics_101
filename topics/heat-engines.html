Heat Engines & Entropy

Machines that convert thermal energy to work, and the fundamental limits on their efficiency.

Theory

A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by operating in a cycle between a hot reservoir (at temperature T_H) and a cold reservoir (at temperature T_C). In each cycle, the engine absorbs heat Q_H from the hot reservoir, performs work W, and exhausts waste heat Q_C to the cold reservoir. By the First Law: W = Q_H − Q_C. The efficiency of the engine is η = W/Q_H = 1 − Q_C/Q_H.

The Carnot engine is a theoretical engine operating with maximum possible efficiency between two temperatures. Its efficiency is η_Carnot = 1 − T_C/T_H (with temperatures in kelvins). No real engine can exceed this efficiency — it is a fundamental limit set by the Second Law of Thermodynamics. To maximize efficiency, you want the largest possible temperature difference. This is why power plants use superheated steam (high T_H) and why engine exhaust is as cool as practical (low T_C). Real engines always have lower efficiency due to friction, irreversible processes, and incomplete heat transfer.

Entropy provides the deepest understanding of why heat engines cannot be 100% efficient. In any real cycle, entropy is produced, and this entropy must be expelled as waste heat. The total entropy change of the universe is always positive: ΔS_universe ≥ 0. This principle explains the arrow of time — why processes naturally proceed in one direction (ice melts in warm water, never the reverse spontaneously). Refrigerators and heat pumps are "reverse" heat engines that move heat from cold to hot, but they require work input and still increase total entropy. Examples of heat engines include car engines (Otto cycle), diesel engines, jet engines (Brayton cycle), and steam turbines (Rankine cycle).
