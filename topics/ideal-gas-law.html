Ideal Gas Law

The equation of state that relates pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of an ideal gas.

Theory

The Ideal Gas Law combines the empirical gas laws of Boyle, Charles, and Avogadro into a single equation: PV = nRT, where P is pressure, V is volume, n is the number of moles, R is the universal gas constant (R = 8.314 J/(mol·K)), and T is absolute temperature in kelvins. This equation describes the behavior of an "ideal" gas — one whose molecules have negligible volume and no intermolecular forces. Real gases approximate ideal behavior at low pressures and high temperatures.

The kinetic theory of gases provides a microscopic explanation for the ideal gas law. Gas molecules move randomly at various speeds, and their collisions with the container walls create pressure. The theory predicts that the average kinetic energy of a gas molecule depends only on temperature: KE_avg = (3/2)k_BT, where k_B = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K is Boltzmann's constant. This means the root-mean-square speed of molecules is v_rms = √(3k_BT/m), where m is the molecular mass. Lighter molecules move faster at the same temperature.

The ideal gas law can also be written as PV = Nk_BT, where N is the total number of molecules. For a fixed amount of gas undergoing a process, the combined gas law states P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂. Special cases include isothermal processes (constant T: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂), isobaric processes (constant P: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂), and isochoric processes (constant V: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂). Understanding gas behavior is essential for thermodynamics, chemistry, atmospheric science, and engineering.
