The EM Spectrum

The full range of electromagnetic radiation from radio waves to gamma rays.

Theory

The electromagnetic spectrum encompasses all electromagnetic waves ordered by wavelength (or equivalently, frequency). All EM waves travel at the speed of light in vacuum: c = fλ = 3 × 10⁸ m/s. From longest wavelength to shortest: radio waves (λ > 1 mm, used for broadcasting and communication), microwaves (1 mm – 1 m, used for cooking and radar), infrared (700 nm – 1 mm, thermal radiation), visible light (400–700 nm, the only part humans can see), ultraviolet (10–400 nm, causes sunburn), X-rays (0.01–10 nm, medical imaging), and gamma rays (λ < 0.01 nm, nuclear processes).

Higher frequency (shorter wavelength) means higher energy per photon: E = hf = hc/λ, where h is Planck's constant. Radio photons have negligible energy, while gamma ray photons can penetrate matter and damage DNA. Visible light occupies only a tiny sliver of the spectrum: red light has wavelength ~700 nm and violet ~400 nm. The Sun's peak emission is in the visible range, which is why our eyes evolved to be sensitive to these wavelengths.

Different regions of the spectrum are produced and detected differently. Radio waves are generated by oscillating charges in antennas. Infrared is emitted by all warm objects. Visible light comes from electron transitions in atoms and hot objects. X-rays are produced by high-speed electrons hitting metal targets. Gamma rays come from nuclear reactions and radioactive decay. Every region has important applications: radio for communication, microwaves for WiFi and cooking, infrared for thermal imaging, UV for sterilization, X-rays for medical diagnostics, and gamma rays for cancer treatment. The spectrum is continuous — the named regions are convenient divisions, not sharp boundaries.
