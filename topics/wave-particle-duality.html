Wave-Particle Duality

The quantum reality where all matter and energy exhibit both wave and particle characteristics.

Theory

Wave-particle duality is the fundamental quantum concept that all entities (light, electrons, atoms) exhibit both wave-like and particle-like behavior. Light shows particle behavior in the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering, and wave behavior in interference and diffraction. In 1924, Louis de Broglie proposed that matter also has wave properties, with wavelength λ = h/p = h/(mv), where h is Planck's constant and p is the momentum. This de Broglie wavelength is negligibly small for macroscopic objects but significant for electrons and other small particles.

The wave nature of electrons was confirmed in 1927 by Davisson and Germer, who showed that electrons scattered from a nickel crystal produced a diffraction pattern, just like X-rays. The double-slit experiment with electrons is one of the most profound demonstrations in physics: when electrons are sent one at a time through two slits, each electron hits the screen at a single point (particle behavior), but over time the accumulated hits form an interference pattern (wave behavior). This pattern appears even though each electron passes through both slits simultaneously as a wave.

The wave function Ψ describes the quantum state of a particle. Its squared magnitude |Ψ|² gives the probability density of finding the particle at a given location. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle states that certain pairs of properties cannot both be known precisely: Δx · Δp ≥ h/(4π). This is not a limitation of measurement instruments but a fundamental property of nature. Wave-particle duality and the uncertainty principle form the foundation of quantum mechanics, which successfully describes the behavior of atoms, molecules, semiconductors, lasers, and all of modern physics and chemistry.
