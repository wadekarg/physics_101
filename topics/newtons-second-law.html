Newton's Second Law — F=ma

The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration.

Theory

Newton's Second Law is the most important equation in classical mechanics: F_net = ma, or equivalently Σ⃗F = m⃗a. It states that the acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This means a larger force produces a larger acceleration, and a more massive object requires more force to achieve the same acceleration. The direction of the acceleration is always in the direction of the net force.

It is critical to distinguish between mass and weight. Mass is an intrinsic property of an object (measured in kilograms) that does not change with location. Weight is the gravitational force on an object: W = mg, where g is the local gravitational acceleration. On the Moon, where g is about 1.6 m/s² (compared to 9.8 m/s² on Earth), you would weigh about one-sixth of your Earth weight, but your mass would be unchanged.

To apply Newton's Second Law, you must identify all forces acting on the object, sum them as vectors to find the net force, and then use a = F_net/m to find the acceleration. In component form: ΣF_x = ma_x and ΣF_y = ma_y. This vector approach is essential for solving problems involving multiple forces at different angles, such as blocks on inclined planes, objects connected by ropes, and many other classic physics scenarios.
