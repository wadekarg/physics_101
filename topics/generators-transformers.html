Generators & Transformers

Converting mechanical energy to electrical energy and efficiently changing voltage levels.

Theory

An AC generator (alternator) converts mechanical energy into alternating current by rotating a coil in a magnetic field. As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux changes sinusoidally, inducing an EMF: EMF = NABω sin(ωt), where N is the number of turns, A is the coil area, B is the field strength, and ω is the angular velocity. The peak EMF is EMF₀ = NABω. This produces alternating current (AC) that oscillates between positive and negative values — the basis of the electrical power grid.

A transformer transfers electrical energy between two coils through a shared magnetic core, using Faraday's Law to change voltage levels. The primary coil creates a changing magnetic flux that induces an EMF in the secondary coil. The voltage ratio equals the turns ratio: V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁. A step-up transformer (N₂ > N₁) increases voltage; a step-down transformer decreases it. For an ideal transformer, power is conserved: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂, so increasing voltage decreases current proportionally.

Transformers are essential for efficient power transmission. Power plants generate electricity at moderate voltage, step it up to hundreds of thousands of volts for long-distance transmission (reducing I²R losses), then step it down at substations for distribution, and finally step it down again to household voltage (120 V or 240 V depending on country). Transformers only work with AC because the flux must change continuously. This is the primary reason AC won over DC in the "War of Currents" between Tesla/Westinghouse and Edison. Real transformers have efficiencies of 95–99%.
