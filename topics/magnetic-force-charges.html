Force on Moving Charges

How magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges and current-carrying wires.

Theory

A magnetic field exerts a force on a moving charged particle given by: F = qvB sin θ, where q is the charge, v is the speed, B is the field strength, and θ is the angle between the velocity and field vectors. In vector notation: ⃗F = q⃗v × ⃗B. The direction is given by the right-hand rule: point your fingers in the direction of ⃗v, curl them toward ⃗B, and your thumb points in the direction of ⃗F (for positive charges). The force is always perpendicular to both the velocity and the field.

Since the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, it does no work and cannot change the particle's speed — only its direction. A charged particle moving perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field follows a circular path with radius r = mv/(qB) (the cyclotron radius). The period of this circular motion is T = 2πm/(qB), independent of speed. If the particle also has a velocity component parallel to B, it traces a helical (spiral) path.

A current-carrying wire in a magnetic field experiences a force: F = BIL sin θ, where I is the current and L is the wire length. This force is the basis of electric motors, loudspeakers, and galvanometers. Two parallel current-carrying wires exert forces on each other: parallel currents attract, antiparallel currents repel. This interaction is actually used to define the ampere in the SI system. Particle accelerators like cyclotrons use magnetic fields to bend charged particles into circular paths, while mass spectrometers use the radius dependence on mass to separate and identify different isotopes.
