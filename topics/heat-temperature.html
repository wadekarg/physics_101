Heat vs Temperature

Understanding the distinction between thermal energy transfer and the measure of molecular motion.

Theory

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. It determines the direction of spontaneous heat flow: from hot to cold. The SI unit is the kelvin (K), related to Celsius by T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. Heat (Q) is the transfer of thermal energy between objects at different temperatures. Heat is not something an object "has" — it is energy in transit. An object has thermal energy (internal energy), but heat refers only to the transfer process.

The amount of heat required to change an object's temperature depends on its mass, the temperature change, and a material property called specific heat capacity (c): Q = mcΔT. Water has an unusually high specific heat capacity (c = 4186 J/(kg·°C)), which is why it is an excellent coolant and why coastal climates are moderate. When substances change phase (melting, boiling), energy is absorbed or released without a temperature change: Q = mL, where L is the latent heat.

Heat transfers by three mechanisms: conduction (direct molecular contact, governed by Q/t = kAΔT/d), convection (bulk fluid motion carrying thermal energy), and radiation (electromagnetic waves, governed by the Stefan-Boltzmann law P = εσAT⁴). All objects above absolute zero radiate thermal energy. Thermal equilibrium occurs when two objects reach the same temperature and net heat flow ceases — this is formalized as the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: if A is in equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C.
