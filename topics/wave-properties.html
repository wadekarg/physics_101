Wave Properties

The fundamental characteristics shared by all waves, from ocean swells to sound and light.

Theory

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy without transferring matter. Waves are characterized by several key properties: wavelength (λ) is the distance between two consecutive identical points (e.g., crest to crest); frequency (f) is the number of complete oscillations per second, measured in hertz (Hz); amplitude (A) is the maximum displacement from equilibrium; and wave speed (v) is how fast the wave pattern moves. These are related by the fundamental wave equation: v = fλ.

Waves are classified as transverse or longitudinal. In a transverse wave, the medium oscillates perpendicular to the direction of wave travel — like waves on a string or electromagnetic waves. In a longitudinal wave, the medium oscillates parallel to the wave direction — like sound waves, which consist of alternating compressions and rarefactions. Some waves, like ocean surface waves, have both transverse and longitudinal components. The period T is the time for one complete cycle: T = 1/f.

The mathematical description of a sinusoidal wave traveling in the +x direction is y(x,t) = A sin(kx − ωt), where k = 2π/λ is the wave number and ω = 2πf is the angular frequency. The speed of a wave depends on the properties of the medium: for a string, v = √(T/μ), where T is tension and μ is linear mass density. For sound in air, speed depends on temperature. Understanding these basic properties is the foundation for all wave phenomena, from music to telecommunications to quantum mechanics.
